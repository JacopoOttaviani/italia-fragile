The bill for neglected prevention

Repairing costs far more than preventing. Since the end of the Second World War, landslides and floods have handed Italy a bill of 112 billion euros, and in recent years the pace has tripled. Securing the entire national territory would cost around 26 billion: little more than half of what has been spent on repairs since 2010.

How much would investing now save?

The European Commission's Joint Research Centre has estimated that every euro invested in river flood-retention basins avoids up to four euros of future damage. Applied to Italy's needs, that ratio would turn the €26 billion of risk-mitigation works into over €100 billion of avoided damage in the decades to come.

Even without multipliers, the maths adds up quickly: securing the entire country costs about eight years of repairs at the current pace. Yet funding keeps arriving mostly after the disasters: according to Greenpeace's analysis of Civil Protection data, between 2015 and 2024 landslides and floods produced over €19 billion in officially recognised damage, and public compensation covered barely 17% of it. The rest fell on families, businesses and local authorities.

And the biggest saving appears in no budget: every project completed before the disaster is a list of victims, evacuees and compensation payments we will never have to count.

Method and sources

How to read the data

Landslides. The areas classified as high (P3) and very high (P4) hazard in Italy's hydrogeological management plans, mosaicked at national scale by ISPRA. This is the indicator used for land, population and buildings exposed.

Floods. The areas liable to flooding under the medium hazard scenario (P2): floods with a return period of 100 to 200 years, as defined by European Directive 2007/60.

The people. The exposed population is the number of residents in hazard areas according to the most recent ISTAT census available on the platform. It is not a forecast: it is a snapshot of who lives today in areas classified as hazardous.

The local detail. The "actual areas" switch overlays the ISPRA mosaics on the map as published, via the IdroGEO WMS service: it is the sub-municipal detail of the classified areas, and it turns on automatically when you search for a municipality.

The limits. The mosaics aggregate plans drawn up at different times and with different criteria by the district basin authorities; boundaries are generalised for display. For official purposes, the maps of the competent authorities prevail.

About the project

ItaliaFragile was born to make a risk that touches nearly every Italian municipality readable by anyone. The data on landslides and floods exist, are public and of good quality — but they remain confined to technical platforms and specialist reports. This atlas turns them into maps, rankings and understandable figures, municipality by municipality, so that citizens, journalists and administrators can see where risk concentrates, who lives inside it, and what it costs to keep chasing disasters instead of preventing them.

Frequently asked questions

Answers to the most common questions about what the atlas shows, where the data come from and how to reuse them.

What exactly does ItaliaFragile show?

The areas classified as high and very high landslide hazard (P3 and P4) and the flood-prone areas under the medium scenario (P2) for Italy's regions, provinces and 7,899 municipalities, together with the exposed population and buildings. The data come from the ISPRA national hazard mosaics published on the IdroGEO platform.

My municipality appears among the hazard areas: what does that mean?

It means that part of the municipal territory is classified as hazardous in Italy's hydrogeological management plans, not that a disaster is imminent. It is a snapshot of where risk exists and is documented: 94% of Italian municipalities have at least one landslide or flood hazard area.

What is the difference between hazard and risk?

Hazard is the probability that a phenomenon — a landslide or a flood — affects a given area. Risk combines hazard with exposure (the people, buildings and activities present) and their vulnerability. The atlas shows hazard and exposure: where the classified areas are and who lives in them.

Where do the data come from and how up to date are they?

The risk indicators come from the ISPRA IdroGEO platform, which mosaics Italy's hydrogeological management plans at national scale and is updated periodically. Casualty data come from the CNR-IRPI Polaris periodic report, administrative boundaries from ISTAT, and the economic figures from ANCE-CRESME, ISPRA ReNDiS and the JRC. All sources are listed under Method & sources.

Can I reuse the maps and the data?

Yes. ISPRA IdroGEO data and ISTAT boundaries are published under a CC-BY 4.0 licence: you can reuse them by crediting the source (ISPRA IdroGEO, ISTAT) and, if you reuse this site's elaborations, ItaliaFragile with a link.
